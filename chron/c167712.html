Monday, 1 December 1677, n.s.

21 November 1677, o.s.

At Paris.

Thursday, 2 December 1677, n.s.

22 November 1677, o.s.

At Paris.

Journal entries: Degree, Countesse of Northumberlands case, Dentium dolor cum convulsionibus, Convulsio [repeated on pages 372, 373, 374, 376] — [Dewhurst]

Letters:

  • From Dr. John Mapletoft, dated 22 November 1677 o.s.; written from Gresham College, London; addressed to Locke at Paris. [Corr., i. 358]
  • From Mrs. Margaret Blomer, written 2 December 1677 n.s.. [Corr., i. 359]

    • Between 6 and 7 p.m., Locke received a note from Mrs. Blomer, summoning him to attend on her mistress, the Countess of Northumberland (wife of the English ambassador), who was suffering from a violent toothache, accompanied by a facial tic [“convulsio”] (diagnosed on the basis of Locke’s case notes as trigeminal neuralgia). Locke attended the Countess for the next two weeks and sent letters to Mapletoft asking him to consult with his London medical colleagues. The case is described in Kenneth Dewhurst, “A symposium on trigenimal neuralgia” (1957).

Persons encountered: Mrs. Margaret Blomer (letter), Dr. John Mapletoft (letter), Elizabeth, Countess of Northumberland (letter)

Friday, 3 December 1677, n.s.

23 November 1677, o.s.

Weather observations at Paris, “[E]xtreemly cold with an exceeding hard frost.” — [Journal, ed. Lough; part shorthand]

Saturday, 4 December 1677, n.s.

24 November 1677, o.s.

At Paris.

Letters: Three letters to Dr. John Mapletoft, all dated 4 December 1677 n.s.; relating to the Countess of Northumberland’s case.

  • A short note failed to arrive by 3/13 December and is now lost.
  • The first surviving letter; written from Paris; addressed to Mapletoft at Gresham College, London; answered by Mapletoft on 3/13 December 1677. [Corr., i. 360]
  • A later letter; written from Paris; answered by Mapletoft on 3/13 December 1677. [Corr., i. 361]

Persons encountered: Dr. John Mapletoft (letters), Elizabeth, Countess of Northumberland (letters)
